# Chapter 05 — "Three"

The first body was found at dawn.

Cole heard about it before he saw it. Haruki moved through the settlement with a particular efficiency that meant something had already failed beyond his help. They found the colonist—name was Marcus, late thirties, civil engineer from Lagos—about 200 meters beyond the perimeter markers, in a clearing where the undergrowth thinned and the humming trees opened to actual sky.

Marcus had walked out at dusk the day before. Nobody noted the exact time. Nobody marked departures yet; the settlement was small enough that you could see who was missing just by looking. Ren found it first when she did evening equipment count. Marcus's workstation was still warm.

They'd searched until dark, then stopped. Going into the forest after dark had become instinct-level stupid. By month one, everyone knew the rules about dark. By day thirteen, Suri had learned them in seizures. By day fourteen, everyone had internalized the lesson.

Cole arrived at the clearing while Haruki was still examining the body. The smell was already present—not rot yet, something else. Sweet and wrong, like overripe fruit left in closed air. Haruki looked up from Marcus's face—or what remained visible of it—and Cole did the quick math of exposure. Eight to ten hours minimum.

"Spore response," Haruki said. His hands were already gloved. Everything about his posture said this was not a surprise, which meant it was bad in a specific way. "Allergic cascade. Throat closure, pulmonary edema. The lower temperature at night activates the spore germination. I've seen similar compounds in the soil samples Dana was analyzing."

Cole knelt. Not to touch—Haruki would have objected—but to observe. Marcus's lips had a blue tint. His hands were clawed, fingers rigid. There was liquid in his airway that had dried to a crust. The man had suffocated fighting for air the planet wouldn't let him take.

"He didn't stay on the marked paths," Haruki said, which wasn't quite an excuse but close. "Wandered into the tall grass. Maybe looking for something. Maybe just walking."

Cole stood. The math was already recalculating behind his eyes, but he didn't let it move his face. Forty-six. No—forty-six. Marcus was gone but the count had to reset. Not 47 minus 1. The new baseline was forty-six, and forty-six was the number he'd now calculate from. This was important. You couldn't count backward. You had to move forward from the number that remained.

"We need perimeter fence," Cole said. "Real fence. Not markers."

Haruki's face suggested he'd been thinking the same thing. "We need to know what kills after dark," he said. "Not just this. The class of threat. If it's not spores—"

"It's the planet," Cole said. "The rules change when the sun's down. Assume everything is hostile until we know it isn't."

It took four hours to rig proper fence. Not the delicate marking he'd set up during the first week—actual barrier. They used pre-fab shelter paneling they'd torn loose, lashed it to the trees that had the most stable root systems. It looked desperate and temporary and like an admission that they weren't safe in the place they'd landed.

By noon, they were looking at perimeter, fence line, and the settlement beyond it. Cole did the work alongside everyone else, not watching from above like a foreman. It was easier to maintain authority if people saw you sweat too.

---

The second death came three days later.

Storm system moved in faster than the weather models predicted. Not a hurricane—the planet's atmosphere didn't seem to generate those kinds of systems—but sustained wind that came down like a hand pressing everything flat. One of the pre-fab shelters, the one they'd repurposed as equipment storage, had a structural joint that failed under sustained lateral pressure. The beam didn't drop cleanly. It twisted, caught on the shelter frame, held for a moment like it was deciding, then came down across a colonist who'd been working inside retrieving supplies.

Her name was Chen. She'd been an accountant. Cole had liked her because she understood margin. She understood the way numbers compressed when you pushed them too far. She understood the cost of precision.

They recovered her body after the wind died. Haruki said she'd died instantly. Cole wasn't sure if that was true or if Haruki was being kind, but he didn't ask for clarification.

The shelter was salvageable. The joint was a design flaw in the pre-fab specifications—load-bearing connection rated for wind speeds fifteen percent below what Parcel 16b actually generated. Cole should have caught it during the equipment inventory. He had caught it. He'd calculated that losing ten percent of shelter capacity was statistically acceptable against the labor cost of reinforcing a shelter designed and manufactured to fail at exactly this pressure. He'd made the choice. Chen had paid it.

Forty-five.

---

The third death was stupider, and that was almost worse.

A colonist named Ridge—also younger, mid-twenties, geologist, enthusiastic and careful—had been scouting a ridge system to the east during clear morning hours. Not explicitly against protocol, but not approved. He'd wanted to map underlying geology, believed he could identify mineral deposits that might help with water sourcing or structural materials. Cole respected the thinking. It was initiative. It was the kind of thing that got you killed on a strange planet.

Ridge had slipped. Nothing exotic. The stone was more fractured than his geological eye had read from a distance. His foot went through a crack that looked solid. He tried to catch himself on a rock edge that crumbled. He fell maybe twelve feet into a ravine that opened up sideways.

The search party found him when the calls for help stopped. It was a straightforward fracture—femur, complicated, bone through skin. Haruki set it as well as he could in field conditions. But infection moved fast here. The planet's bacterial ecosystem was just different enough from Earth's that the antibiotics in their medical kit treated it like it was optional. By day four, the fever was untouchable. By day six, Ridge was gone.

Haruki sat with him at the end. Cole watched from outside the medical shelter, not intruding. Haruki's job was to witness death. Cole's job was to count it.

Forty-four.

---

Cole called the colony together at dusk on the day they cremated Ridge. Not a memorial. Memorials took time they didn't have. This was a logistics briefing. He stood in front of forty-three other humans and held the numbers in the air between them.

"Forty-four alive," he said. "Forty-seven landed. Three dead in four weeks. At this rate, we're looking at nineteen dead by month twelve. That leaves twenty-eight. The target is twenty-four."

Someone shifted weight. Someone else breathed out like they'd been holding it.

"The clause requires twenty-four to own the land," Cole continued. "Twenty-four original signatories alive at the five-year mark. Right now we're on track to hit that by month eight and then dip below. After that, every person who dies is a person we can't afford to lose."

Ren Tsai stood up. Her face was sharp, engineer's problem-solving mode.

"People aren't margin," she said. "We're not calculating our own deaths."

Cole met her eyes. This was the conversation. This was always going to be the conversation.

"They are if you want them to be alive," he said. "The math is the only thing that keeps us breathing. You reject the numbers, you're just hoping. Hope doesn't keep anything running."

"Hope is what makes this worth surviving," Ren said. "If we're just running calculations, we might as well be machines."

"I'd be fine with that," Cole said. "Machines don't lose people they care about."

The word *care* sat in the space between them for a moment. Cole hadn't planned to say it. It just fell out of his mouth like a stone he'd been carrying.

Ren didn't answer. She sat down.

Around the settlement, people processed the two statements in real time. Some nodded at Ren—the ones who wanted to believe that survival was about more than arithmetic. Some nodded at Cole—the ones who understood that arithmetic was the only honest language left. Most of them nodded at both, which meant they were thinking clearly and that was the worst possible outcome.

Later, in his shelter, Cole pulled out the modified tablet where he kept his running calculations. The numbers were already there, updated each morning. He stared at them until the digits stopped making sense and just became shapes.

Forty-four.

Twenty-four.

Twenty in five years.

The problem was simple math. The problem was the numbers were people. The problem was that Ren was right and he was right and the planet didn't care about either one of them.

He put the tablet away and lay in the dark and counted the hum of the trees, which never changed and never stopped, and thought about how being alone on a dying colony was exactly what he'd come here for.
