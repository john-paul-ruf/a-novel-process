# Chapter 06 — "Ghost Colony"

The scouting party found it by accident.

Ren had led a group west to map potential fresh water sources—the mineral content in their current reserves was shifting and Dana wanted a fallback location. They'd marked a rough grid, moved methodically through the forest, counted steps and measured declination. Standard surveying work. Cole wasn't with them. He was managing shelter reinforcement back at the settlement. He learned about the discovery from Joss Carver, who'd been trailing at the rear of the expedition and moved through the settlement looking like his skeleton wanted to escape his body.

"You need to see it," Joss said. No preamble. "Like, immediately. We're not telling anyone yet. Ren said wait for you."

The site was twelve kilometers west, deeper into the forest than Cole had approved for surveying runs. The first marker was a piece of pre-fab shelter paneling, half-absorbed by creeping vegetation. Then another. Then a small clearing where the spiral trees had been deliberately cut or had failed—hard to tell which—and the undergrowth had reclaimed the space with brutal efficiency.

Four shelter units, collapsed in various states of partial excavation by the planet. The aluminum frames were still visible, still Asterlyne's logo, but older. Previous generation. The manufacturing dates on the frame stampings read seven to eight years old. Parcel 16b had been attempted before.

Cole moved through the clearing without touching anything. Ren watched him process. Joss kept looking at the sky like he expected something to drop from it.

The equipment was mostly useless. Corroded harvest tools. Water collection systems rusted to their component minerals. A drone with its solar cells clouded beyond function. All of it was technically salvageable and all of it was worthless. The planet had spent seven years teaching these machines that staying here meant becoming part of the geology.

There was a shelter that hadn't fully collapsed. The door mechanism had seized, but the structure held. Cole worked the door open with a tool Ren handed him. Inside: dust and organized decay. Medical supplies in waterproof containers. Data tablets. A water filtration system that looked like it had been maintained until maintenance stopped mattering. Food storage units empty and lined with what looked like fungal residue.

And, in a sealed case bolted to the interior wall, a data core.

Cole opened it. The seal had held—environmental protocols working correctly even after seven years of submersion in a hostile atmosphere. The core was a standard Asterlyne model, encrypted and dated. Cole didn't try to access it on the spot. He just held it.

"This is a previous expedition," Ren said. Not a question.

"Fourteen colonists," Cole said. He was reading the manifests on the interior wall, printed on material that had survived the weather better than the metal had. "Came in on charter designation XR-Seven. Launch date seven years, four months ago."

"They failed," Joss said. Not a guess. Fact-finding.

"Completely," Cole said.

He pocketed the data core without asking permission. Ren didn't object. They marked the site's coordinates, made a note in the survey log—casual, like they'd found a previous water source they could revisit—and walked back to Holdfast without talking.

That night, Cole sat in his shelter and accessed the core.

The logs were clinical at first. Mission parameters. Daily resource inventory. Weather observations. Elena Vasik was the expedition lead, and she wrote like a scientist—precise, minimal, focused on data that mattered. Her first month entries looked almost identical to Cole's month one observations. Soil chemistry fluctuation. Equipment insufficiency. Three deaths in the first month. Spore response, structural failure, a fall during scouting.

Three in one month. Cole had hit the same number by month one. He kept reading.

Elena's second month got tighter. Shorter entries. The clinical language started to fray at the edges. More questions. Less answers. Three more deaths. One expedition member vanished into the forest and was never recovered. The colony was down to ten.

By month three, Elena's entries were sparse. Word economy had shifted from efficiency to exhaustion. She was rationing everything including language. The creep appeared. The previous expedition didn't have a Dana to map its patterns. They tried fire. It accelerated the spread. They tried chemicals. Worked temporarily. Then the creep adapted. Five more deaths across months three and four, mostly from exposure in attempts to contain the biological advancement. The colony was down to five.

Month five was the last full entry Elena had written. She was alone by then. The others had either died or done something Elena didn't record—just noted their absence with a blank line and a date. Her final paragraph was still clinical, which made it worse:

"Asterlyne knows the second-year cycle. They chose not to tell us. I found it in the geological survey metadata, buried in an appendix no one was meant to read. The planet operates on multi-year orbital oscillation. The first year is setup. The second year is hostile. The third through fifth are cycling pressure—relief, then pressure again, scaled down. They knew this when they sent us. They documented it in data they didn't pass to us. The supplies they sent are designed to function in Year One conditions. Year Two will strip them down to useless. By the time the second cycle truly emerges, there's no way to call for help because the communication systems will be failing at the same rate as the biological environment."

The log ended mid-thought. The next file was a single audio recording, two words in a female voice—the audio quality degraded, but the words were clear:

"Enough, now."

Cole closed the core and sat in the dark.

He pulled his own data package—the one Asterlyne had provided at charter signing. His own geological survey metadata. His own appendix. He scrolled through it looking for the section on orbital cycles. Found it. Twenty pages of dense climate modeling, seasonal projections, multi-year oscillation patterns, everything Elena had described.

Then nothing. The appendix ended abruptly. The next section was formatted oddly, file-size misaligned. Cole searched the document properties. The appendix had been edited. Pages removed. Seven pages, based on the section number gaps. The climate data that showed the second-year hostile phase had been redacted from his copy before he signed the charter.

His copy. Not Ren's. Not Dana's. His.

The settlement would have different versions, Cole realized. Different holes, different redactions, depending on what Asterlyne needed from each person. The engineer might get different information than the doctor. The botanist might get different information than the logistics officer. The colonists weren't receiving one charter—they were receiving forty-seven different charters, each one with different lies.

He thought about Garrett Polk reading the shortened version. About Joss Carver working from a document that might have explained why building systems of anything seemed to fail catastrophically. About Dana Wen making decisions based on data that was already half-missing.

Asterlyne hadn't sent them to an undocumented planet. They'd sent them to a documented planet and deleted the documentation. The company had run the numbers. The planet was going to be hostile. The colony was going to fail. And if some of them survived through pure mathematics and luck, Asterlyne could always point to the charter and say the colonists had been informed. Look. It's right here. Redacted. But it's still right here.

Cole took the data core back to the sealed container it had come from. Didn't tell anyone what it said. Just brought it back to the settlement and put it in his shelter and read Elena's logs every night for a week, looking for something he'd missed. Some instruction. Some solution she'd failed to see.

She'd found the second-year cycle in the metadata.

She hadn't found a way to survive it.

---

By week three of month two, Cole had compartmentalized the ghost colony into a fact, which meant he could function. The colony was forty-four. The target was twenty-four. Elena's colony had started with fourteen and gotten down to one. Cole was running different math. The variables were different. The outcome didn't have to be the same.

He still carried Elena's final recordings in his head. The way she'd documented death like it was just another data point. The way her voice had fractured when she stopped pretending to be a scientist and just recorded two words into the dark.

He thought about telling Ren. Thought about it in the way you think about things you know will change everything, and then you decide to wait one more day, and one more day becomes a week becomes a month becomes something you're doing alone at night in a shelter that hums with the forest.

He began to study the geological appendix the way Elena had. Began to cross-reference it against Dana's soil sampling. Began to understand what the second year would actually look like.

By the end of month two, Cole knew something Elena hadn't: he had a botanist who could map the cycles before they happened. He had time to prepare. He had the ghost colony's death toll as a roadmap of what not to do.

The question was whether knowing where you'd fail made it possible to fail differently. Or whether you just fell in a new direction, the same speed, hitting different ground at the bottom.

Forty-four alive.

Fifty-five months remaining.

The humming trees didn't answer.
