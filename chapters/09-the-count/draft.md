# Chapter 09 — "The Count"

Six months. Cole stood in the settlement's makeshift administrative shelter—a reinforced pre-fab with a table that held the colony's maps, equipment manifests, work rotation schedules, and the running tally of who was alive and what they could do with their bodies and skills. He'd been coming here at dusk most days, reviewing the day's data, adjusting projections, doing the invisible work that made it seem like the settlement ran on something other than mathematics and fragile luck.

Thirty-nine.

He'd started with forty-seven. Three in the first month, one from Sarah's resistant infection, then a construction accident that was really a cascade—exhaustion leading to misjudgment leading to a fall that would have been recoverable if the planet didn't have its own way of turning minor injuries into exit routes. The count had been narrowing for two months now, each death a number he had to subtract and then carry with him like a constant weight.

But thirty-nine was holding. The agricultural cycle was stable. Dana's breakthrough had loosened the rationing enough that people weren't eating at a permanent deficit. Ren's council system was working—or not working, but functioning in the way that systems do when everyone involved has agreed to pretend the framework matters. Garrett Polk had been neutralized, which was a word Cole used in his head without examining it too closely. Polk had wanted to signal for rescue. Ren had offered him a place on the council—real input, real voice in the decisions that affected the colony. In exchange, Polk dismantled the signal array. Not because he'd changed his mind about the situation, but because the promise of agency was enough to make him believe he had options again.

It wasn't democracy. Cole knew that. Democracy required actual equality. What they had was informed consent to a hierarchy that wore the shape of shared decision-making. But the colonists had accepted it. Some because they believed in it. Some because the alternative was isolation. Most because the settlement had enough work to keep everyone distracted.

Cole closed the day's reports. The shelter was dimming as the sun moved toward the horizon. He had maybe twenty minutes of useful light left. Enough time to eat something or do something useful. He chose neither. He chose to sit at the table with a piece of paper and the knowledge that he owed a message to a daughter who didn't know she'd been abandoned on an alien world.

---

The message had to be simple. Cole understood that much. Lily was nine. Nine-year-olds had attention spans measured in paragraphs, not pages. He'd tried to write something the day after landing and stopped after the first sentence. Everything that came after felt like a lie or an admission, depending on how he tilted the words.

He started with the most honest thing he could think of:

*Lily,*

*I'm on a planet very far away. The trees here are unlike anything on Earth. They have spiral trunks that twist as they grow, and they hum at a frequency you feel more than hear. At night, when the wind moves through them, the sound reminds me of—*

He stopped. Reminds him of what? Her mother singing? A lullaby he'd stopped playing years ago? He deleted the line.

*The trees here hum. The sound is below human hearing, but you can feel it in your chest if you stand under them long enough. It's beautiful and strange and I think you'd want to draw it. You like drawing things that don't exist on Earth. You draw horses with wings. You draw castles that float. This planet is full of things that would be impossible in your sketchbook, except they exist, so maybe the sketchbook was the universe's way of preparing you.*

He stopped again. Too much. Too trying. Cole knew how to talk in his own head. Talking to Lily was different. She was real and distant at the same time. More real for the distance.

He started over.

*Lily,*

*I'm on a planet very far away. The trees are beautiful. I wish you could see them.*

That was true. That was the most useful truth he had. He folded the message and set it aside. Three sentences. It had taken him two hours to arrange them so they didn't sound like a man saying goodbye.

He didn't know when the message would reach Earth. The relay satellites had their own orbital mechanics. The ship that would eventually carry it back had its own schedule. Months, probably. Maybe longer. Lily would read it whenever it arrived. By then she'd be older. The trees would still be here. Cole might not be.

He pressed his thumb against his eye and held it there until he stopped thinking about any of that.

---

The temperature dropped as the sun set. Cole had learned to read the settlement's microclimates—which shelters held heat best, where the wind tunneled, when to expect the thermal inversions that made breathing feel deliberate. But tonight was different. The drop was sharper than the daily cycle predicted. Three degrees below the historical average by his calculation. Then four. Then five.

Dana appeared at his shelter opening like she'd been waiting for the signal that something had changed. Maybe she had been. The settlement had become a system where people responded to environmental shifts the way a body responds to temperature: automatically, without conscious decision.

"Soil samples," she said. "I need to run them now. There's been a shift in the overnight read."

Cole was already on his feet. They moved toward the lab without talking. The forest around them was changing audibility—the hum that was always present was deepening, finding lower frequencies. Cole had read that sound travels differently in different media. He wondered if the planet was speaking and they were only now developing the ears to hear it.

Dana went directly to the analysis equipment. No preamble. No exhaustion. She was in the mode that Cole recognized as her truest self—problem reduction, data collection, the specific peace that came from having a concrete question to answer.

The samples took thirty minutes to process. Cole stood at the perimeter of the lab and watched the numbers populate on her screen. The patterns that Dana had mapped so carefully last month—the predictable cycles—they were still there. But underneath them was a new variable. Temperature shift. Mineral composition changing in a direction she hadn't tracked before. Bacterial colonies that should have been dormant beginning to activate.

When she turned from the screen, her face had shifted into something that Cole recognized as the expression she wore when the data told her a story that contradicted her previous understanding.

"It's starting," she said. "The second cycle."

Cole didn't ask what that meant. He was already pulling Elena Vasik's Year Two logs from where they'd been sitting unopened on his shelf. He'd reached the boundary of Elena's Year One three days ago. Everything after had been territory he hadn't wanted to enter. The previous expedition had survived six months. Elena had been confident at the six-month mark, just like Cole was supposed to be confident now. And then something had turned. Elena had used the phrase "second cycle" in her Month Nine entry.

Cole opened the data core.

Elena had written: *The planetary rotation created seasonal variations. Our survey missed this. The first orbital cycle brought the conditions that allowed terrestrial organisms to flourish. The second cycle is different. The flora is entering dormancy. The fauna is becoming more active. The planet is shifting, and we are not equipped to shift with it.*

Cole closed his eyes and held that sentence in the dark behind his eyelids.

They'd made it six months. They'd built systems. They'd created a margin. They'd discovered that the margin was temporary. And now the planet was turning the page again, and everything they'd learned was about to become outdated.

"How long?" he asked Dana.

"For the shift to complete? The data on the previous cycle suggests two to three weeks. After that, we're in unknown territory."

Cole opened his eyes. Thirty-nine people. A food system based on crops that were about to refuse to grow. Shelter systems calibrated for the first cycle's weather patterns. Water sources that depended on snowmelt from mountains that were about to stop seeing regular precipitation.

He'd calculated survival against a five-year timeline. He'd optimized every system for maximum efficiency within the constraints. He'd thought about margin and ratio and acceptable loss.

He hadn't calculated for a planet that changed the rules every few months.

"We need to go back to the full council," Dana said. "Tonight if possible. They need to understand—"

"I know what they need to understand," Cole said. His voice came out colder than he'd intended. "They need to understand that everything they've learned is about to stop being true. They need to understand that the stability they felt over the last month was a temporary gift. They need to understand that five years on this planet might mean five different systems, five different solutions, five different versions of survival."

"So we tell them that," Dana said. "We don't lie."

Cole looked at her. She was covered in soil again. She never entirely cleaned it off. The planet was becoming part of her at a cellular level, he thought. Maybe it would be easier if everyone just accepted that. Accepted the transformation as inevitable.

"I'll call the council meeting," he said. "You present the data. Make it clear. Make it specific. Make it so they can't pretend it's theoretical."

Dana gathered her notes. Cole stayed in the shelter as the darkness outside became complete. The trees were humming louder now. He could hear it even with the shelter walls between him and the forest. The planet was singing the transition. Not welcoming the colonists into the new cycle. Just announcing that it was happening whether they were ready or not.

Thirty-nine people.

Six months down. Fifty-four more to go.

Cole opened Elena's logs and read every entry from Month Seven onward, preparing himself for the story that came next.
