# Chapter 11 — Creep

The thing appeared on Month 8, Day 3, as a gray-brown smear at the forest edge, twenty meters beyond the perimeter markers.

Cole noticed it during dawn inventory, a discoloration in the lavender understory that hadn't been there the previous evening. The soil around the settlement had been shifting all month — temperatures dropping, drainage patterns changing, the whole planet recalibrating for its second pass through the orbital cycle. A new discoloration was one more variable in a system already too full of them. He logged it. Moved on.

By Month 8, Day 7, it had expanded to three meters.

By Day 10, it was a band ten meters wide and advancing at a rate Cole could measure against marker stones. Slow. Patient. A centimeter an hour, maybe less. He'd set Ren to charting it — time-marked photographs every four hours, distance measurements, soil samples from the leading edge. The thing moved like erosion, like time made visible.

It was Dana who named it. "The creep," she called it in the weekly resource meeting, and the word stuck because it was accurate. It crept. That was all it did — moved forward with no apparent urgency, breaking down everything organic in its path.

Haruki collected samples. Ran them past his makeshift field lab. Came back with observations: fungal network of some kind, but nothing matching Earth taxonomy. Mineralized base structure, like the substance in the ancient channels but active, flowing, consuming. It dissolved plant matter on contact — the way mold breaks down a corpse, except faster, more complete.

Elena had written about the creep. Cole pulled her Year One logs that night and found the entry dated Month 9: *"The fungal bloom has accelerated since we began tracking it. Initial theory: seasonal response to orbital position. The structure maintains internal moisture and temperature regulation — it's not dying back. It's feeding."*

Elena's next entry was Month 11: *"We attempted containment with fire. The accelerant ignited, but the creep adapted — routed around the burn, moved deeper into the soil. The burned section appears to have accelerated its growth rate. Chemical barriers purchased from Asterlyne inventory: effective for 4-6 weeks. After that, adaptation. The thing learns."*

Month 13: *"The creep has consumed the eastern shelter line. We're relocating again. Running out of room."*

Cole closed the logs and walked to the perimeter to watch it move.

It was beautiful in a way that made him uneasy. The texture was almost organic — a network of filaments so fine they looked like lace from a distance, except the lace was dissolving the soil beneath it, breaking down the biomass left by the second-cycle plant die-off. The color was gray-brown-lavender, shifting in the low light. It didn't move like a wave. It moved like breath. Expansion and rest, expansion and rest.

"It's not attacking," Dana said, coming up beside him. She had a specimen container in one hand, a measurement tape in the other. She'd spent three days collecting samples, running composition analysis on the compound structure. "It's decomposing."

"It's advancing on the settlement."

"It's doing what it's evolved to do. The second cycle brings dormancy, die-off, dead matter. The planet has a cleanup system." Dana crouched at the creep's edge, studying the gradient where the fungal network met unaffected soil. "If we were a forest, we'd welcome this. Nutrient cycling. Renewal."

"We're not a forest."

"No. But we're in one. There's a difference."

Cole understood the distinction intellectually. It didn't change the math. At current advancement rate, the creep would reach the perimeter shelters in seventy to eighty days. The core settlement — where the medical facility was, where the food stores were, where most of the colonists slept — was another two hundred meters inland. Ninety, maybe a hundred and ten days. Call it four months. Call it the time until the second cycle deepened and nothing was predictable anymore.

He brought it to Ren.

"We need to relocate," she said. Not a question. A statement she'd already mapped the implications of.

"We need to understand it first. Elena tried containment. Fire didn't work. Chemicals bought them six weeks. After that—"

"After that, they abandoned the site and lost the agricultural work they'd already done. We don't have Elena's luxury. We're at thirty-nine. She started with fourteen."

Cole didn't correct her. She meant Elena was running the math differently — fewer people meant earlier desperation meant faster decisions. Ren's logic was sound. Elena's colony had mobility. The Free Colony was rooted.

Dana led them deeper into the research, setting up a makeshift lab in one of the cleared shelters. She collected samples from different points along the creep's boundary, analyzed the internal structure under magnification (a repurposed engineering microscope, never meant for biology), tracked the compound's response to various external stimuli. Ren helped. Joss Carver volunteered to assist, ferrying equipment, taking notes with the concentration of someone trying to prove his utility.

It took Dana two weeks to map the pattern.

The creep's composition shifted with depth. The surface layer was aggressive — breaking down plant matter at acceleration. The middle layers were transitional, the filaments more tightly woven. The deepest sections, several centimeters down, were crystalline. Mineralized. Dormant.

"It's storing," Dana said, showing Cole the cross-section. The compound was sealed in a transparent container, and under the magnification lens, the internal structure was almost geometric. "Look at the ratio of active to inert material. The active processes break down the biomass, and then the mineral structures preserve and store what it's extracted. It's not just decomposing. It's sequestering. Building something."

"Building what?"

"I don't know yet. But it's intelligent in the way a system can be intelligent. It's responding to the planet's needs, not expressing malice. It's not our enemy, Cole. It's the planet's immune system. It just happens to be cleaning a space we're occupying."

The distinction was fine enough that it didn't matter. The creep was still advancing on the settlement. The colonists could appreciate the elegance of its design while simultaneously understanding that they had ninety days to move everything they'd built to a location it would need another hundred days to reach.

Polk wanted to burn it again. Cole vetoed the idea without explanation — Elena had already provided the data that fire accelerated growth, and Polk hadn't read Elena's logs. The man wanted action, immediate and decisive. Cole wanted a solution that would hold. Those two wants collided in the daily council meetings, where Polk's frustration mounted and Cole's silence only deepened it.

On Month 9, Day 2, one of the perimeter shelters ceased reporting.

It was a routine check — the supply tech who worked the eastern shelter line didn't show for the morning count. Cole sent a search team. They found the shelter intact but unsealed. The opening in the wall had been gradual — the creep had grown through the composite material, patient and methodical, following the path of least resistance. The colonist — a man named Marcus Reeves, thirty-one, former software engineer who'd taken the settlement logistics role because it kept him occupied and away from processing why he'd come to a dying planet in the first place — was still inside.

Haruki did the field examination. Cole watched from a respectful distance, close enough to understand, far enough to preserve something like dignity for what remained. The creep had been indiscriminate. Reeves's lower body was dissolved to the point where the internal structure was visible — not in a clinical, educational way, but in a way that made clear the fungal network considered organic matter as raw substrate. His upper body was intact. His face was untouched. His eyes were closed, or dissolved to where it appeared they were closed. Haruki said, "He was unconscious before the significant dissolution occurred. The creep would have released compounds — chemical breakdown preceding physical degradation. He wouldn't have felt it, not acutely, not at the end."

It was a mercy Haruki was offering: not truth, maybe, but kindness dressed as medical assessment.

Cole helped Haruki carry what was left to the processing area. They didn't call it cremation — the planet's high-temperature cycles and the shelter's heating system could serve the same purpose. Haruki prepared the body with the same precision he applied to everything, which was the only dignity available: careful, methodical, without performance or false ceremony.

No one saw the body but Haruki. Cole kept it that way, not through explicit instruction but through presence — staying near the processing shelter, making it clear that this particular space was closed. The colonists understood. They always did. Death was common enough that they'd learned the etiquette of it.

The count dropped to thirty-eight.

That night, Cole pulled Elena's logs and re-read the sections on creep containment with the specificity of a man trying not to repeat another person's extinction. Elena had documented everything: the fire attempt, the chemical application, the timeline of adaptation and advance. Her conclusion, written in Month 14 after the third relocation: *"The creep isn't something we can fight. It's something we can only move ahead of. Every settlement we establish becomes a temporary barrier — the creep consumes the organic substrate we've established and then continues. We are not the problem. But we occupy the problem's path."*

Three relocations. Elena had moved her colony three times. The third relocation was made with six people remaining. The creep had consumed the other eight.

Cole did the math on the map. The ancient channels ran higher, on rock substrate. Less organic material. If the creep was a decomposer, it would struggle in a mineral-rich environment. The settlement could relocate to the channels, build on stone instead of soil, strip the organic matter from the perimeter. It was possible. Elena hadn't found this option, or had found it too late to execute.

Cole brought the plan to Ren.

"We have ninety days," he said.

"We have seventy," Ren corrected. "After that, the creep reaches the northern agricultural plots. Once we lose the food production zone, the timeline accelerates. Shortages, morale collapse, decision-making degrades. We move before Day 70."

"The creep isn't even moving at full speed yet. If the second cycle intensifies—"

"It will. And we'll be in the process of relocation when it does, carrying everything we've built on our backs, moving a population that's increasingly malnourished and frightened. Cole, I'm not being pessimistic. I'm reading the pattern." She laid out the timeline on a map, marked the creep's current position and projected advancement. "We have two weeks to decide. Four weeks to plan. Eight weeks to move everything. After Day 70, it's emergency extraction, and we lose infrastructure."

Cole understood what she wasn't saying: we lose the people who can't move fast.

He went to the ancient channels alone and stood in the carved stone, listening to the planet's underground systems, the subtle sound of water and sediment and time working. The channels ran east-west, parallel to the settlement. A four-kilometer walk would establish a new position on the higher ground, rock substrate, defensible perimeter. The channels themselves could provide water. The platforms near the channels had enough cleared space for shelter construction. It was habitable. It was survivable.

It meant abandoning the structures they'd built, the agricultural experiments, the systems they'd refined over eight months of learning the planet's rhythms. It meant starting over, which was its own kind of dying.

Cole stood there for an hour, maybe more, watching the darkness gather in the channels. The creep would reach this site eventually, in two years or three. But by then, the colonists would have moved again. And again, if they lived. The planet was a system in constant transformation. Survival meant accepting that home was temporary, that every settlement was a pause, that the creep would always be somewhere beyond the perimeter, patiently advancing, consuming the past to make room for the future.

He walked back to the settlement and called the council together.

"We vote," he said. "We relocate or we stay. Either way, we decide together."

Ren nodded. Garrett Polk stood up.

"I vote we move," Polk said. "I'm done watching this place take pieces of us."

The vote was held the next day. Thirty-eight colonists cast thirty-eight votes for relocation.

Cole should have felt relief. Instead, he felt the weight of Elena's logs, the finality of knowing that one of the thirty-eight wouldn't make it through the move, and that he'd already done the math on which ones were most likely to break.
