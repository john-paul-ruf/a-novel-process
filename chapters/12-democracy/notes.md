# Chapter 12 — Author Notes

## Voice Decisions

Ren's POV shifts the narrative perspective from Cole's internal calculation to Ren's external observation and problem-solving. While Cole does math inside his head, Ren *does* the work in the world. The chapter's voice reflects this: more action-oriented sentences, more movement between physical spaces, more coordination of logistics rather than strategic analysis alone. Ren's interiority is less about feeling and more about pattern recognition — she notices Patricia's cough as a *data point*, James's injury as a *cascading failure*, Noor's death as a *political cost*, the channels as *architecture*.

The emotional temperature darkens because Ren doesn't have Cole's calculation shield. Her observation of Cole doing the math after Thomas dies ("Cole had already subtracted him from the thirty-eight and begun recalculating margins") is more damning than Cole's own interiority could be, because it's filtered through Ren's recognition of the cost. Noor's death — one of the seventeen who voted to stay — adds a dimension Cole's POV couldn't access: the irony of dying for a plan you opposed.

## Structural Notes

Chapter 12 compresses the six-day relocation process through a combination of scene and summary:
- Beat 1 (Day One): Agricultural harvest, conversation with Dana
- Beat 2 (Night 1): Thomas Chen's death (38→37)
- Beat 3 (Night 3): Noor's exposure death (37→36)
- Beat 4 (Days 4-5): Compressed relocation work, James's compound fracture and infection
- Beat 5 (Day 6): Discovery of ancient channels; creep confirmed not following to rock substrate
- Turn: Conversation with Cole about inheritance vs. homesteading

The chapter doesn't re-dramatize the vote from Chapter 11 because that work is already done. Instead, it focuses on execution — the material reality of moving thirty-eight people and everything they've built four kilometers through hostile terrain under time pressure. Each death and injury is presented matter-of-factly, without sentimentality, because these are the expected costs of the plan Cole already calculated.

The ending pivot from relocation logistics to architectural discovery signals a thematic shift: the colony is beginning to understand that they're not creating a settlement, they're inheriting one. This complicates the ownership math that's central to the novel's tension.

- **Ancient channels**: Identified as deliberately built, not natural. The mineralized deposits are deliberately structured. This is significant for understanding the planet's indigenous infrastructure
- **The problem shift**: Relocation solves the creep problem (channels on rock substrate) but introduces a new problem: understanding what the planet's previous inhabitants were doing and whether their infrastructure can be used/integrated by the new colony

## Off-Voice Flags

RESOLVED — Cole's dialogue about the channel builders tightened. Removed the enumeration ("consumed them or they left or they failed") in favor of a more direct, characteristically terse dismissal ("Channels are interesting. They're not food, shelter, or medicine."). Ren's counter ("They might be all three") sharpens the exchange.

The chapter tracks the voice profile closely:
- Medium sentences with short declarative punches ("Thirty-six." "The problem was that knowledge and execution were different objects.")
- Deep interiority for Ren (her observation of Dana, her recognition of what Cole is doing mentally, her reflection on Noor and democracy at the end)
- Pragmatic dreamer tone: the relocation is necessary and grim, but Ren's response is to organize it with precision rather than despair
- Dialogue is sparse and functional (Dana on adaptation, Cole on whether they're homesteading)
- Dark warmth: Haruki's mercy with Thomas, the respect between Ren and Dana, Cole and Ren's non-romantic but deep mutual recognition
- Noor's death line ("Noor had died doing the thing she'd voted against, and the math of that was worse than the cold") carries the voice's characteristic irreverent observation
