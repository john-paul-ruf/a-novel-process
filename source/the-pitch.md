# The Pitch — THE FREE COLONY

## Logline

A disgraced logistics manager signs a one-way corporate charter to homestead a hostile exoplanet, knowing the company expects most colonists to die — but if he can keep twenty-four of the original forty-seven alive for five years, a buried clause grants them ownership of the land and a second chance he stopped believing he deserved.

## One-Page Synopsis

### Beginning

Cole Maren, 38, has lost his daughter, his career, and every reason to stay on Earth. When Asterlyne Corp opens a homesteading charter for Parcel 16b — an exoplanet with marginal viability data — Cole reads the contract the way no one else bothers to. The terms are predatory. The company is buying death data: how humans fail on a foreign world, packaged into actionable intelligence for future (profitable, corporate-controlled) colonization. But clause 9.4.2 says what Asterlyne never expected anyone to survive long enough to invoke: if fifty-one percent of original signatories are alive at the five-year mark, the land transfers to the colonists in perpetuity. Cole does the math. Twenty-four out of forty-seven. He signs.

The forty-seven arrive on Parcel 16b — a world that is beautiful the way a riptide is beautiful. Breathable air, viable soil, forests of spiral-trunked trees that hum at frequencies just below hearing. But the viability data told a half-truth. The planet operates on biological rhythms the surveys barely captured: soil chemistry shifts with the seasons, native organisms are territorial in ways that don't map to Earth logic, and the weather patterns punish anyone who assumes yesterday's safety holds tomorrow. Within the first month, three colonists are dead.

Cole's logistics instincts make him the reluctant leader of a colony that didn't elect him. He organizes supply chains from nothing, rations the equipment Asterlyne sent (insufficient, by design), and begins learning the planet's rules through loss. Meanwhile, the colonists fracture into factions: those who want to signal for rescue and abandon the charter, those who want to stay but resent Cole's authority, and the quiet ones who are just trying not to die.

Among the dead colony's wreckage — because Parcel 16b was attempted once before, a fact Asterlyne buried — Cole discovers expedition logs from a previous charter group. Fourteen colonists. None survived Year Two. Their records become a ghost narrative: a warning, a map, and eventually a mirror.

### Middle

By Year Two, the count is thirty-one. Cole is holding, but barely. The planet's second-year biological cycle is worse than the first — an organism the colonists call "creep" begins encroaching on their settlement, a slow-moving fungal network that dissolves organic material on contact. The previous expedition's logs describe it. They didn't solve it. Cole has to.

The internal fractures deepen. Maren Tsai, an engineer who signed the charter to escape Earth's surveillance economy, emerges as a rival voice — not antagonistic to Cole, but philosophically opposed. She wants democracy. Cole wants efficiency. They're both right, and the argument nearly tears the colony apart during the creep crisis.

Then the supply drops stop.

Asterlyne's corporate communications shift tone — subtle, lawyered, deniable. Cole suspects what the previous expedition's logs confirm: the company doesn't want them to succeed. A successful charter invocation means Asterlyne loses the planet. Cole realizes someone among the colonists may be reporting back — feeding Asterlyne intelligence on their vulnerabilities.

The body count climbs. Twenty-eight. Twenty-six. The margin narrows. Cole, who built his life on calculations, begins to feel the weight of each number as a face, a voice, a person he ate breakfast with. The logistics manager is becoming something else. He starts telling the younger colonists about Earth — not the Earth he fled, but the one worth remembering. He starts talking about his daughter, Lily. Not to them, exactly. To the version of himself that might deserve to see her again.

The previous expedition's logs reach their final entries. The last colonist alive wrote: "The planet isn't killing us. We stopped being worth keeping alive." Cole reads it three times. He understands.

### End

Year Four. Twenty-four colonists remain. Exactly the threshold. No margin for error. One more death and the math fails.

Cole identifies the corporate informant — not through investigation, but because she confesses. Dana Wen, a quiet botanist who signed a separate contract with Asterlyne: report back on colony operations and her debt on Earth is erased. She's been feeding information for three years. She's also the one who figured out how to neutralize the creep. The colony's survival and its betrayal live in the same person.

The colony votes. Not Cole's decision — Maren Tsai's democracy, finally enacted. They vote to let Dana stay. The margin holds.

Asterlyne makes a final play: a corporate shuttle arrives at the start of Year Five with a "renegotiation offer." Leave now, debts forgiven, no questions asked. The catch is implicit — if they stay and even one person dies before the five-year mark, they get nothing. The shuttle is a dare.

Cole speaks to the colony. Not a speech — he's never been that person. He shows them the math. The real math: what Asterlyne has extracted from every failed colony, what Parcel 16b is worth on the resource market, what their lives have been converted into on a balance sheet. Then he shows them clause 9.4.2.

Nobody boards the shuttle.

The five-year mark passes on a Tuesday. No ceremony. Cole is repairing a water filtration line when Maren Tsai tells him it's done. He sits on the ground outside the filtration shed, in the dirt of a planet that spent five years trying to kill him, and writes a message to his daughter. Not "come here." Not yet. Just: "I built something. When you're ready, there's a place."

## Theme

Freedom has always been a math problem — who counts, what counts, what's an acceptable loss. But the math breaks down when the numbers become people. *The Free Colony* is about the moment a man who survived by calculating learns that the thing worth building can't be optimized. It's about systems designed to extract value from human desperation, and the stubborn, irrational, deeply human refusal to let them.

It is also, quietly, about fatherhood — not as a biological fact but as a practice. Cole doesn't earn his daughter back. He earns the right to try.

## Comparable Titles

- *The Martian* by Andy Weir — survival through competence and problem-solving on a hostile world, but with a full ensemble cast and systemic stakes beyond one person's rescue
- *The Long Way to a Small, Angry Planet* by Becky Chambers — found family in space, warmth earned through shared hardship, characters who feel like people you'd eat lunch with
- *Station Eleven* by Emily St. John Mandel — rebuilding civilization from wreckage, the tension between survival and meaning, nested narratives that illuminate each other

## Structural Notes

- **POV:** Third-person limited, past tense. Primarily Cole, with 2-3 secondary POV characters (Maren Tsai, Dana Wen) for structural breadth.
- **Nested narrative:** The previous expedition's logs function as a story-within-the-story, discovered and read by Cole across the novel. Fragments, not full chapters — integrated into the main narrative the way a person reads a journal: in stolen moments, out of order, with growing dread.
- **Timeline:** Five years compressed into three parts. Part One (Months 1-6, ~30%). Part Two (Months 6-30, ~35%). Part Three (Years 3-5, ~35%). Time jumps handled through interiority — Cole's internal landscape marks the passage of time more than calendar dates.
- **Target length:** 80,000-100,000 words.
- **Content:** KDP non-mature. Violence is survival-contextual, not gratuitous. Language is naturalistic (moderate profanity). No explicit sexual content. Darkness comes from systemic exploitation and moral pressure, not graphic content.
